## Overview

This repository contains the data, code, and outputs for the empirical analysis of how US funding market conditions transmit to corporate credit spreads. The analysis uses Granger causality with HAC-robust inference across two complementary frameworks:

- **Section 3 (Price and Collateral Channels):** Tests whether the SOFR-EFFR spread, reserve balances, TGA flows, and ON RRP predict changes in Moody's Aaa and Baa credit spreads. Uses the post-2018 sample (Apr 2018 - Apr 2026, ~2,004 daily obs).

- **Section 4 (Dollar Channel):** Tests whether the US dollar index (DXY) predicts ETF-based credit measures (HYG/LQD, HYG/SHV) and emerging market bonds (EMB), linking domestic funding plumbing to the global dollar funding channel (Bruno and Shin, 2015). Uses the extended sample (Dec 2007 - Apr 2026, ~4,544 daily obs).

Both sections include rolling window analysis, reverse causality checks, COVID-19 sensitivity tests, residual diagnostics (Breusch-Godfrey, ARCH-LM), and lag selection robustness.

## Key Findings

1. **The SOFR-EFFR price channel is episodic.** Under HAC-robust inference, it activates only at longer lags (5-10 days) and during stress episodes (September 2019, March 2020), not continuously.

2. **The reserve quantity channel operates through Aaa, not Baa.** Reserves predict Aaa spread changes (safe-asset substitution channel), while Baa spreads are unaffected. Significance weakens under HAC correction.

3. **The dollar is the strongest predictor of credit risk measures.** DXY Granger-causes EMB at all lags under HAC ($p < 0.001$), and HYG/SHV at the weekly frequency. The relationship is bidirectional and survives COVID exclusion.

4. **Both channels are regime-dependent.** Rolling window analysis shows predictive power concentrates during stress windows, supporting the interpretation that funding-credit transmission is an episodic mechanism.
